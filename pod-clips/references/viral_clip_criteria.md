# What Makes a Viral Short-Form Clip

## Selection Criteria

When scanning a transcript for clip-worthy moments, look for sections that hit multiple of these signals:

### Content Signals
- **Contrarian take** — Speaker says something that goes against common belief. These spark debate and shares.
- **Surprising fact or number** — "$10K check became $100 million", "8 years in stealth with thousands of employees"
- **Universal pain point** — Something the audience has personally experienced and feels strongly about.
- **Vivid analogy or metaphor** — A complex idea made simple through comparison. These get screenshotted and shared as quotes.
- **Origin story moment** — The "how it started" beat that makes the audience feel like insiders.
- **Vulnerability or honesty** — Speaker admits failure, fear, or uncertainty. Authenticity cuts through noise.
- **Actionable insight** — A framework, mental model, or principle viewers can immediately apply to their own life.

### Delivery Signals
- **High energy shift** — Speaker suddenly gets animated, leans forward, raises voice.
- **Natural storytelling rhythm** — Speaker builds tension, pauses, delivers. Not just listing facts.
- **Humor** — Genuine laughs, self-deprecating jokes, witty one-liners.
- **Quotable lines** — Phrases that work as standalone text overlays or tweet-length quotes.

### Structural Signals
- **Self-contained** — The moment makes sense without any prior context from the interview.
- **Clear beginning and end** — Not a fragment ripped from the middle of a larger point.
- **Appropriate length** — 30–120 seconds. Capture the full story arc, but don't let it sprawl. A complete 90-second clip that gets condensed later is better than a gutted 40-second fragment. Hard ceiling at 2 minutes (120s) — if a story needs more than that, find a tighter entry point or split it into two clips.

## What to Avoid
- Sections that are just listing facts or features without narrative
- Inside references that require context from earlier in the conversation
- Long rambly tangents that don't reach a clear conclusion
- Segments where the speaker is clearly searching for words or losing their train of thought
- Anything that requires visual context (pointing at something off-screen, referencing a slide)

## Clip Count Guidelines

As a baseline, aim for roughly **1 clip per 7–8 minutes** of source content. A 60-minute interview should yield ~8 clips. But quality over quantity — if you only find 5 great moments in an hour, that's better than padding with 3 mediocre ones. Conversely, a particularly rich interview might yield more.
