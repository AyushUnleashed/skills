# Clip Cutting Rules

These rules apply to every clip cut from a long-form interview or podcast. A clip is a **mini-story** — it must stand alone and make complete sense to someone who has never seen the full video.

## 1. Every clip is a story: HOOK → SETUP → PAYOFF

The first 3 seconds must grab attention. The middle builds tension or context. The last line must land like a punchline or period — never a comma. If you can't identify all three parts, the clip isn't ready.

## 2. Never start mid-answer from a prior question

Read 10–15 lines BEFORE your intended start in the transcript. If the speaker is still finishing a thought from a different topic, your start is too early. The viewer has zero context — they must immediately understand what's being discussed.

**Test:** Read ONLY your clip's text out loud, starting from second 1. If the first sentence feels like it's responding to something you haven't heard — move the start forward until the speaker introduces the topic themselves.

## 3. Never cut before the punchline

Read 10–15 lines AFTER your intended end. Look for the "button" — the final phrase that makes the audience feel something. Common button patterns:
- A callback to the opening hook
- A mic-drop one-liner that summarizes everything
- A vivid metaphor or image
- A laugh line or self-aware joke

If the best line is 5–10 seconds past your cut, extend. A 90s clip with a complete story beats a 50s clip that ends mid-thought.

## 4. Prefer complete arcs over tight duration

Don't optimize for short duration. Optimize for complete thoughts. A clip should feel like it has a beginning, middle, and end — like a paragraph, not a sentence fragment.

**Rule of thumb:** If removing the last 10 seconds makes the clip feel unfinished, those 10 seconds are essential.

**Important:** Capture the WHOLE story. A 2-minute clip with a complete narrative arc is far better than a 40-second fragment that lost its setup or context. Duration tightening should never happen during the initial clip extraction. You can always condense later, but you can never recover context you didn't capture.

**Hard ceiling: 2 minutes (120 seconds).** If a story runs longer than that, find a tighter entry point or split it into two clips.

## 5. The "scroll test" for the opening

Imagine a viewer scrolling Reels. They land on second 1 of your clip. Within 3 seconds they must:
- Know WHAT topic is being discussed
- Feel compelled to keep watching

If the first 3 seconds are "yeah", "um", or the tail of a different answer — the viewer is gone.

## 6. Check for dangling context

Before finalizing a clip, ask:
- Does this reference something said earlier that the viewer won't have? (e.g., "That's why we did it" — did what?)
- Does the speaker say "as I mentioned" or "going back to"? These signal you're mid-conversation, not at a clean entry point.
- Are there pronouns without antecedents? ("This is what made it work" — what is "this"?)
- Would a stranger watching this clip on mute with captions understand the first sentence?

## 7. Let the energy guide the cut

The speaker's energy often signals natural story boundaries:
- **Energy rise** = good start (speaker leaning in, voice getting louder)
- **Energy peak** = middle of the story (don't cut here)
- **Energy drop / laugh / pause** = natural end (cut here or 1–2 beats after)

Cutting during a peak feels abrupt. Cutting during a valley feels natural.

## 8. Read the full surrounding context (30 lines each direction)

Before locking any timestamp, always read at least 30 lines before your start and 30 lines after your end in the transcript. This prevents:
- Starting in the tail of a prior answer
- Missing a better hook a few seconds earlier
- Cutting before the real punchline
- Including a dead spot that kills momentum
